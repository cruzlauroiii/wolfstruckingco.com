Port of LA Drayage: Complete Guide 2026

Published April 10, 2026 · 8 min read · By Wolfs Trucking Co.

If you're importing or exporting through the Port of Los Angeles or Port of Long Beach, drayage is the link between your ocean container and your warehouse. Get it wrong and you're paying detention, demurrage, chassis fees, and broker markups. Get it right and you're paying $285 for a 22-mile haul.

This guide covers everything shippers need to know in 2026: how drayage works, who to call, what to pay, and the 5 most common mistakes that cost you money.

What Is Drayage?

Drayage is the short-distance trucking of ocean containers between a port terminal and a nearby warehouse, intermodal yard, or distribution center. For LA/Long Beach imports, that typically means 15-70 miles from the terminal to destinations like Commerce, Fontana, Ontario, or Riverside.

Why Drayage Is Specialized

Drayage isn't regular trucking. It requires:

  • TWIC-certified drivers — Transportation Worker Identification Credential for unescorted port access
  • Chassis management — the platform under the container (different pools, different fees)
  • Terminal appointments — Pier Pass (PierPass/TMF) scheduling required
  • Container seal verification — chain-of-custody documentation
  • Demurrage tracking — avoiding last-free-day penalties

The 13 Terminals at Port of LA & Long Beach

There are 13 major container terminals across both ports. Which one you use depends on your ocean carrier.

Port of Los Angeles Terminals

  • APM Terminals (Pier 400) — Maersk, MSC, CMA CGM
  • TraPac (Berth 136-147) — ONE, Mitsui OSK Lines

  • Fenix Marine Services (Pier 300) — CMA CGM, ANL

  • West Basin Container Terminal — various

Port of Long Beach Terminals

  • Pier J — ITS (International Transportation Service)
  • Pier G — ITS / various
  • Pier F — various
  • Pier T (TTI) — Total Terminals International, Hanjin heritage
  • SSA Marine Pier A — various
  • LBCT Middle Harbor (Pier E/F) — Long Beach Container Terminal, OOCL

Demurrage: The Hidden Cost

Demurrage is the fee ocean terminals charge when your container sits past its "last free day" (LFD). In 2026, demurrage at LA/LB runs $180-$250 per day per container — and it compounds.

How to Avoid Demurrage

  1. Track your LFD — Know the exact day before fees start
  2. Book drayage early — appointments fill fast, book 48 hours before LFD
  3. Confirm terminal appointment — verify Pier Pass slot matches your container
  4. Have destination ready — warehouse needs an appointment too, not just the port
  5. Use a drayage carrier with live container tracking — see exactly where your box is

The 5 Most Expensive Drayage Mistakes

Mistake 1: Using a broker when you have consistent volume

If you move more than 5 containers/month on similar routes, a direct carrier relationship saves 15-25%. Brokers are for one-off or emergency loads.

Mistake 2: Not tracking chassis fees separately

Chassis pool fees can add $25-$75 per container if you're not with a carrier that provides chassis. Always verify chassis is included in the quote.

Mistake 3: Missing terminal appointments

A missed Pier Pass appointment means re-scheduling, which can push your pickup 24-48 hours. Your drayage carrier should handle this, not you.

Mistake 4: Paying for "fuel surcharge" separately

Fuel should be in the base rate. If a carrier bills fuel separately, they're likely also charging broker markups. Red flag.

Mistake 5: Not negotiating detention hours

Most drayage includes 2 hours free at pickup and delivery. If your warehouse takes longer, make sure the detention rate is pre-agreed (industry standard: $50/hour).

TWIC: What Shippers Should Know

TWIC (Transportation Worker Identification Credential) is required for any driver entering a port terminal. If your drayage carrier doesn't have TWIC-certified drivers, they can't pick up your container — period.

Ask your carrier:

  • What % of your fleet has TWIC? (should be 100% for drayage-focused carriers)
  • Do drivers have Hazmat (H) endorsement? (required for hazmat containers)
  • How do you handle chassis at terminals that don't pool?
